Wellbeing — The Non-Negotiables
😴

Sleep

Memory consolidates during sleep. Aim for 8–9 hours. Late-night cramming undermines everything revised that day.

☕

Breaks

Short, regular breaks prevent cognitive fatigue. Step away from screens. A short walk beats scrolling every time.

🏃

Exercise

Even 20–30 minutes of physical activity improves focus, mood and memory retention. It is not time wasted.

📵

Environment

Phone in another room. Notifications are the enemy of deep focus. Background music without lyrics is fine for some learners.

    How to use the Pomodoro Technique

    1
    Choose your taskPick one topic or subject before you start. Have your notes, Knowledge Organiser or past paper ready.
    2
    Focus for 25 minutesWork on nothing else. Phone away. Notifications off. If a thought pops up, write it down and return to it later.
    3
    Take a 5-minute breakStep away from your desk. Stretch, have a drink, look out the window. Do NOT scroll social media.
    4
    After 4 cycles — long breakTake 20–30 minutes away. You've earned it. This is when memory consolidation begins.
    💡 Tip: Use the first 5 minutes of each Pomodoro as a brain dump — write everything you can remember about the topic before opening your notes.

      Exam Technique Guide ✍️
      WJEC-specific advice on how to answer different question types and maximise your marks in the exam room.

      ⏱️ Time Management in the Exam

      The most preventable way to lose marks is running out of time on questions you could answer. Before you start, divide the time available by the total marks to get your "mark-a-minute" rate.

      • Read the whole paper in the first 2 minutes — identify the questions you're strongest on.
      • Don't spend 15 minutes on a 3-mark question. Move on and come back.
      • Leave 5 minutes at the end to check for blank spaces and silly errors.
      • On WJEC papers, the marks in brackets tell you exactly how much to write.

      🔍 Reading the Question

      Most marks are lost not because pupils don't know the answer, but because they didn't answer the question that was asked.

      • Underline the command word: Describe, Explain, Evaluate, Assess, Analyse, Compare.
      • Underline the topic focus — what specifically is the question asking about?
      • Describe = say what it is. Explain = say what it is AND why. Never confuse the two.
      • Evaluate/Assess = you must reach a supported judgement. Don't just list points.

      ✏️ Presentation and Accuracy

      • Write in sentences for any question worth 3 marks or more.
      • Spell key subject terminology correctly — examiners notice.
      • If you make a mistake, cross it out neatly with a single line. Don't scribble.
      • If you run out of space, ask for extra paper — don't squash writing into margins.

      📝 How to Answer Extended Writing Questions 6–12 marks

      Extended writing questions are where the most marks are won and lost. WJEC rewards structured, argued responses — not lists of bullet points.

      • Plan first — always. Even 2 minutes of planning produces a far better answer than diving straight in.
      • Write your plan as a quick list of your main points — don't spend time making it neat.
      • Each paragraph should make one clear point, support it with evidence, and explain its significance.
      • For evaluation questions, you must present both sides and reach a conclusion.
      • Don't just end with "In conclusion, there are many factors..." — make an actual judgement.
      PEEL paragraph structure Point — State your argument clearly in one sentence.
      Evidence — Quote, statistic, named example, or case study.
      Explain — Why does this evidence support your point?
      Link — Connect back to the question or signpost your next point.

      ⚖️ Evaluation and "Assess" Questions

      These questions want you to weigh up evidence and reach a supported decision. Many pupils describe both sides but forget to actually evaluate them.

      • Use language like: "This is significant because...", "However, this is limited by...", "The most important factor is... because..."
      • Your conclusion must make a clear judgement — not "both sides have valid points."
      • Stronger answers explain why one side is more convincing than the other.

      📄 Source/Document Questions History, English, RS

      Source questions test your ability to use and evaluate evidence — not just describe what it says. WJEC mark schemes specifically reward provenance analysis.

      • Use the source — quote or paraphrase from it directly. Don't just write about the topic.
      • Analyse don't describe — say what the source suggests/implies, not just what it says.
      • Provenance = who created it, when, why, and for what audience. Always consider this.
      • Ask: does the provenance make the source more or less useful/reliable?
      • Use your own knowledge to support, challenge or contextualise the source.
      Useful sentence starters "This source suggests that... which implies..."
      "The fact that this was written by [author] in [date] means that..."
      "This source is useful because... however it is limited by..."
      "This is supported by my own knowledge that..."

      🔢 Calculation Questions Maths, Science, Business

      Calculation questions follow predictable patterns. Most marks are method marks — you can still score even with a wrong final answer if your working is shown.

      • Always show your working — never just write the answer.
      • Write the formula first, then substitute values, then calculate.
      • Include units in your answer — a number without a unit is often marked wrong.
      • If you get a strange answer, check whether you've used the right formula and the right units.
      • For multi-step calculations, label each step clearly so the examiner can follow your method.
      Method to follow every time 1. Write the formula: e.g. Speed = Distance ÷ Time
      2. Substitute: Speed = 120 ÷ 3
      3. Calculate: Speed = 40
      4. Add units: Speed = 40 m/s

      📊 Graph and Data Questions

      • When describing a trend: state the direction, quote specific figures, and identify any anomalies.
      • Don't just say "it goes up" — say "it increases from X to Y between [point A] and [point B]."
      • For gradient calculations: choose two points far apart on the line for accuracy.
      • Always check axis labels and units before answering.

      ✅ Short Answer Questions 1–3 marks

      Short answer questions seem simple but are easy to drop marks on through vagueness or missing key terms.

      • 1-mark questions want one precise point — not a sentence of waffle.
      • 2-mark questions usually want a point plus a brief explanation or two separate points.
      • 3-mark questions typically want a point, explanation, and example or development.
      • Use subject-specific vocabulary — general language rarely earns full marks.
      • If a question says "give one example" — give one. Two won't earn extra marks.

      🔑 Key Command Words — Quick Reference

      • State / Give / Name / Identify — One word or short phrase. No explanation needed.
      • Describe — Say what something is or what happens. No "why" required.
      • Explain — Say what AND why. Use "because", "therefore", "this means that."
      • Analyse — Break it down. Examine the parts and how they relate.
      • Evaluate / Assess / Discuss — Weigh up both sides. Reach a supported judgement.
      • Compare — Identify similarities AND differences. Don't just describe each separately.
      The Night Before & Exam Day 📋
      What you do in the 24 hours before an exam matters more than most pupils realise. Follow this guide to give yourself the best possible chance.
      🌙 The Night Before
      • 📚Light review only. Do a quick brain dump of key topics — no new learning, no cramming. Your brain needs time to consolidate, not more information.
      • 🎒Pack your bag now. Pens (at least two), pencil, ruler, calculator (if allowed), student ID, water bottle. Don't leave this to the morning.
      • ⏰Set two alarms. Don't rely on one. Know exactly what time you need to leave and work backwards.
      • 📵Put your phone away by 9pm. Screens suppress melatonin and delay sleep. The hour before bed should be calm — read, shower, listen to music.
      • 😴Aim for 8–9 hours sleep. Sleep is when memory consolidation happens. A well-rested brain outperforms a tired brain that crammed until midnight every time.
      • 🍽️Eat a proper meal. Your brain runs on glucose. Don't go to bed hungry and don't skip breakfast in the morning.
      ☀️ The Morning Of
      • 🍳Eat breakfast. Even if you're nervous and don't feel like it. Something slow-release — porridge, eggs, toast. Avoid high-sugar foods that cause an energy crash.
      • 💧Drink water. Even mild dehydration affects concentration and memory. Take a water bottle into the exam.
      • 🚶Leave early. Rushing to an exam raises cortisol (stress hormone) and impairs performance. Arrive with at least 10 minutes to spare.
      • 📖Quick review — key terms only. A short look at your revision notes or flashcards is fine. Don't try to learn anything new on exam morning.
      • 🤝Be careful with friends. Avoid classmates who are panicking — anxiety is contagious. Stay calm and focus on yourself.
      • 🧘Breathe. If you feel nervous, take slow deep breaths — in for 4, hold for 4, out for 4. This activates the parasympathetic nervous system and genuinely calms you down.
      📝 During the Exam
      • 👀Read the whole paper first. Spend the first 2 minutes scanning all questions. Identify the ones you're most confident on and start there.
      • ⏱️Watch the clock. Check the time at the halfway point. Are you on track? Don't spend too long on any one question.
      • ✏️Plan extended answers. Even a 60-second bullet-point plan produces a better answer than diving straight in.
      • 🔄Attempt everything. Never leave a question blank. A guess or partial answer can still earn marks. Nothing is lost by trying.
      • 🔍Check your work. If you finish early, check for blank spaces, missing units, and questions you may have missed on the back of a page.
      • 💬Ask if unsure. You can always raise your hand and ask the invigilator to clarify something about the paper. It's allowed.
      ✅ After the Exam
      • 🚫Don't do a post-mortem. Picking over every answer you gave is damaging and pointless. You cannot change it now.
      • 🗣️Limit the debrief with friends. A quick chat is fine. An hour of "what did you put for question 7?" helps nobody and raises anxiety.
      • 🍕Do something you enjoy. A proper break between exams is not lazy — it's essential. Your brain needs recovery time.
      • 😴Prioritise sleep. If you have another exam tomorrow, sleep matters more than squeezing in extra revision tonight.
      • 👨‍🏫Talk to someone if you're struggling. If an exam goes badly, speak to your teacher or a trusted adult. One exam is not the end of the world.

      😰 If You Panic in the Exam — What to Do

      1
      Put your pen down. Stop trying to write. Trying to force an answer while panicking makes it worse.
      2
      Breathe — box breathing. In for 4 counts, hold for 4, out for 4, hold for 4. Repeat three times. This is not wasted time — it works.
      3
      Move to a different question. Skip the question that's blocked you. Come back to it later — often the answer surfaces once the pressure is off.
      4
      Write something — anything. Even a partial answer, a definition of a key term, or a relevant fact. Partial marks are still marks. A blank scores zero.
      5
      Remember: everyone finds it hard. The exam is designed to challenge. Feeling uncertain does not mean you're failing.
